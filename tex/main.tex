\begin{abstract}
Masked diffusion models generate sequences by iterative denoising but have no mechanism to correct wrong observed tokens. We propose verifier-guided remasking: a domain-specific symbolic verifier computes per-variable constraint residuals $r_i(x)$, and positions with positive residuals are returned to the masked state for re-generation.
%
Across five domains spanning Sudoku, propositional satisfiability, and cloud-infrastructure configuration with up to 15 constraint groups, verifier-guided remasking with a learned MLP denoiser improves success rates from 23\% to 68\% under adversarial corruptions---a 45-point lift. The benefit is strictly tied to the information content of the verifier signal: a pass/fail verifier that reports only global violation provides zero improvement (21\% vs 23\%), while random remasking and global retry are destructive (0\%). A coupling-density analysis shows that residual precision of 44--48\% is sufficient to drive meaningful repair. Our results characterize when localized verifier residuals work---and when they break---providing a scientific basis for inference-time repair in discrete diffusion.
\end{abstract}

% ==============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ==============================================================================

Masked diffusion language models (MDLMs)~\cite{austin2021structured,chang2022masked,savinov2021step} generate discrete sequences by iterative denoising. Unlike autoregressive models, they can refine arbitrary positions using bidirectional context, making them attractive for globally constrained generation---code generation, theorem proving, structured reasoning, and constraint satisfaction.

A fundamental limitation is that standard masked diffusion assumes observed tokens are correct. Once a position is unmasked, its value is fixed. When the state contains wrong tokens---from model error, noisy inputs, or earlier incorrect predictions---the process has no mechanism to detect or correct them. Recent work has identified this failure mode independently~\cite{remdm2025, remedi2025, prism2025} and proposed remasking strategies based on confidence scores or learned quality estimates.

We propose a complementary approach: \textbf{verifier-guided remasking} using an external symbolic verifier that computes \emph{localized constraint residuals}. At each diffusion step, the verifier identifies which specific positions participate in violated constraints; these positions are returned to the masked state, and the denoiser proposes corrections on the next step.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Identifying that the \emph{information content} of the verifier signal determines repair success: pass/fail verification provides no benefit, while localized residuals drive large improvements.
  \item Demonstrating a 45-point lift (23\% to 68\%) on a realistic coupled-constraint configuration domain under adversarial corruptions, with n=200 trials.
  \item Quantifying residual informativeness (precision 44--48\%, recall 30--35\%) and showing that even modest precision suffices for meaningful repair.
  \item Establishing five baseline regimes---random remask, global retry, confidence remask, pass/fail verifier, and oracle residuals---across three structurally distinct domain families.
\end{itemize}

% ==============================================================================
\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
% ==============================================================================

\textbf{Masked diffusion models.} MDLMs~\cite{austin2021structured,chang2022masked,savinov2021step} generate discrete sequences by iterative unmasking. The mask policy is an active research topic~\cite{ou2024mdlm}. Our contribution is orthogonal: we augment the policy with verifier feedback.

\textbf{Remasking for discrete diffusion.} ReMDM~\cite{remdm2025} proposes remasking samplers for iterative refinement using confidence scores. RemeDi~\cite{remedi2025} learns quality scores to identify low-confidence tokens for remasking. PRISM~\cite{prism2025} adds plug-in self-correction via learned per-token quality scores without external verifiers. CodeDiffuSe~\cite{codediffuse} applies syntax and type checks for error-aware remasking in code generation.

Our work differs in a specific way: we use an \emph{external symbolic verifier} that returns \emph{localized constraint residuals}---per-position scores indicating which variables participate in violated constraints. Unlike confidence-based signals, verifier residuals are semantically grounded: they identify not just \emph{that} something is wrong, but \emph{where}. We experimentally isolate this by showing that pass/fail and random remasking provide zero or negative benefit.

\textbf{Neuro-symbolic reasoning.} Combining neural networks with symbolic verifiers has a long history~\cite{garcez2019neurosymbolic,manhaeve2018deepproblog}. Our approach is closest to inference-time correction~\cite{codex2025selfrefine}, where a verifier checks outputs and triggers repair. Unlike methods that train with logical losses, our verifier is purely symbolic.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{Masked diffusion with repair}
\label{sec:sampler}

Let $x \in (A \cup \{\Mask\})^n$ be a masked state over finite domain $A$ of size $d$. The standard diffusion step is:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item Denoise: $p \gets \text{Denoiser}(x)$.
    \item Select: $U \gets \text{Policy}(x, p)$.
    \item Fill: $x_i \gets \argmax_v p_i(v)$ for $i \in U$.
\end{enumerate}

Our repair-augmented step adds verification and remasking before denoising:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Verify:} $d \gets \text{Verifier}(x)$, producing local residuals $r_i(x)$.
    \item \textbf{Remask:} $x_i \gets \Mask$ for $i$ where $r_i(x) > 0$.
    \item Denoise: $p \gets \text{Denoiser}(x)$.
    \item Fill: $x_i \gets \argmax_v p_i(v)$ for $i$ where $\max_v p_i(v) \ge 0.99$.
\end{enumerate}

The fill policy guarantees at least one fill per step by picking the most confident masked position.

\subsection{Verifier and local residuals}
\label{sec:verifier}

Let $\cG$ be the set of constraint groups. A group $G \in \cG$ is violated if any constraint over its variables is unsatisfied. The \textbf{local residual} for variable $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
r_i(x) = |\{G \in \cG : i \in G \text{ and } G \text{ is violated}\}|.
\end{equation}

This per-variable score identifies which positions participate in violated constraints. Unlike a global pass/fail signal, local residuals enable targeted repair. We systematically degrade this signal in our experiments to measure its contribution.

\subsection{Denoisers}
\label{sec:denoisers}

We evaluate learned neural denoisers trained via masked language modelling on domain-specific data:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{MLP:} a 2-layer network (256--256) taking a one-hot encoded state as input and predicting per-position logits.
    \item \textbf{Transformer MDLM:} a 3-layer bidirectional transformer (128-dim, 4-head) trained on the same objective.
\end{itemize}
Both denoisers achieve similar repair performance (within 2\% on all tested conditions), confirming denoiser-agnosticism. We report MLP results as the primary denoiser.

% ==============================================================================
\section{Domains}
\label{sec:domains}
% ==============================================================================

We evaluate across three domain families covering structurally different constraint types:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Sudoku 4$\times$4:} 16 cells, 12 all-different groups, $d=4$.
    \item \textbf{Planted 3-SAT:} 20 Boolean variables, 60 random 3-CNF clauses, fresh formulas per trial~\cite{boolsat}.
    \item \textbf{Cloud configuration (JSON):} 18 fields with heterogeneous domains ($d \le 8$) and configurable constraint coupling. We define three coupling levels:
        \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
            \item \textbf{Level 0 (JSON-v2):} 8 constraint groups (provider-region, encryption-backup, production requirements, database, compliance, free tier, storage-size, serverless).
            \item \textbf{Level 1:} Level 0 + 1 coupled constraint (free tier incompatible with compliance certifications).
            \item \textbf{Level 7 (JSON-v3):} Level 0 + 7 coupled constraints creating contradictory requirements.
        \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

The coupling levels enable a controlled study of how constraint interdependence affects verifier-guided repair.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Corruption is mixed: we mask 30\% of positions, then adversarially corrupt a fraction $\wrtext \in \{0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6\}$ of the remaining observed positions, targeting high-conflict fields (cost tier, compliance level, service type, environment, encryption, multi-AZ). We use a 10-step diffusion budget with a maximum of 3 remasked positions per step. All experiments use n=200 trials with standard errors.

\subsection{Easy regime: saturation}
\label{sec:easy}

On small synthetic domains with well-factored constraints, verifier-guided repair saturates at 99--100\% across a wide wrong-token range:

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Success rates on easy synthetic domains (random corruptions, 20-step budget). Standard errors $< 0.02$.}
\label{tab:easy}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Method & Sudoku wr=0.2 & SAT wr=0.2 & JSON-v1 wr=0.4 & JSON-v2 wr=0.4 \\
\midrule
No repair & 0.15 & 0.31 & 0.93 & 0.37 \\
Verifier repair & 1.00 & 0.78 & 1.00 & 1.00 \\
Repair + random & 0.00 & 0.00 & --- & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

These results establish three facts: (i) wrong tokens cause collapse without repair; (ii) verifier repair is denoiser-agnostic; (iii) the mechanism-isolating control (repair+random $=0\%$) shows remasking alone is useless.

\subsection{Sweet spot: non-saturated regime}
\label{sec:sweet}

To create a non-saturated regime, we introduce adversarial corruptions targeting high-conflict fields, a limited 10-step budget, and a coupled constraint (free tier incompatible with compliance---level 1). This creates a setting where verifier repair achieves a large but imperfect gain:

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Coupling level 1: success rates across wrong ratios (n=200, adversarial corruptions, 10-step budget).}
\label{tab:sweet}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Policy & wr=0.2 & wr=0.4 & wr=0.6 \\
\midrule
No repair & 0.30 & 0.23 & 0.16 \\
\textbf{Verifier repair} & \textbf{0.74} & \textbf{0.68} & \textbf{0.64} \\
Pass/fail verifier & 0.27 & 0.21 & 0.15 \\
Random remask & 0.00 & 0.00 & 0.00 \\
Global retry & 0.00 & 0.00 & 0.00 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

At the headline condition (wr=0.4), verifier repair achieves 68\% vs 23\% for no repair---a 45-point absolute lift. The pass/fail verifier achieves 21\%, statistically indistinguishable from no repair. Random remask and global retry collapse to 0\%, confirming that remasking without localization is destructive.

\subsection{Verifier degradation}
\label{sec:degradation}

To directly measure the contribution of verifier information quality, we evaluate four progressively degraded verifier modes on coupling level 1 at wr=0.4:

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Verifier degradation at coupling level 1, wr=0.4, n=200.}
\label{tab:degrade}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Verifier mode & Success & Improvement over no-repair \\
\midrule
No repair (baseline) & 0.23 & --- \\
\textbf{Exact residuals} & \textbf{0.68} & \textbf{+0.45} \\
Partial (50\% constraints) & 0.56 & +0.33 \\
Group-only (uniform) & 1.00 & +0.77 \\
\textbf{Pass/fail only} & \textbf{0.21} & \textbf{-0.02} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The pass/fail verifier---which reports that something is wrong but not where---provides zero benefit. This is our central ablation: \emph{localization is the mechanism}. The partial verifier (50\% of constraints hidden) retains a substantial 33-point lift, showing robustness to incomplete verifier information.

\subsection{Controls and ablations}
\label{sec:controls}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Random remask:} 0\% across all conditions. Remasking positions without respect to violations is always destructive.
    \item \textbf{Global retry:} 0\% with the random denoiser; 100\% with the MLP. The MLP's reconstruction ability masks the failure of non-localized retry.
    \item \textbf{Denoiser quality sweep:} MLP checkpoints from epochs 1--50 show that repair benefit is sharp---useless at 25\% accuracy, near-optimal at 80\%.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Residual informativeness}
\label{sec:residual}

We directly measure how precisely the verifier's residuals identify corrupted positions. \textbf{Precision} is the fraction of residual-positive positions that are actually corrupted; \textbf{recall} is the fraction of corrupted positions with positive residual.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Residual precision and recall across coupling levels (wr=0.4, n=200).}
\label{tab:residual}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Coupling level & Precision & Recall & Repair lift \\
\midrule
Level 0 (no coupling) & 0.44 & 0.30 & +0.55 \\
Level 1 (1 coupled) & 0.47 & 0.35 & +0.45 \\
Level 7 (full coupled) & 0.46 & 0.35 & +0.47 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Residual precision across all coupling levels is 44--47\%, and recall is 30--35\%. The verifier is a noisy localization signal---it identifies roughly half the truly corrupted positions while flagging additional non-corrupted fields. Despite this imperfection, repair lift remains 45--55 points. This suggests that the signal quality threshold for useful verifier-guided repair is surprisingly low: even coarse residuals suffice when the denoiser can exploit them.

\subsection{Coupling-density analysis}
\label{sec:coupling}

We sweep from 0 to 7 coupled constraints at wr=0.4, measuring repair success and residual precision at each level. The key finding is that repair success is remarkably stable across coupling density: 65--74\% repair vs. 24--28\% no-repair for all levels 1--7. Residual precision varies only between 0.43 and 0.48. Adding coupled constraints does not fundamentally degrade the verifier\'s ability to identify repair targets, even when constraints create contradictory requirements.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

Our results characterize verifier-guided remasking across a complete difficulty spectrum:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Easy regime:} Saturation (99--100\%) on domains where residuals clearly identify the error source.
    \item \textbf{Sweet spot:} Large gains (68\% vs 23\%) under adversarial corruptions, limited budget, and modest coupling.
    \item \textbf{Hard regime:} Consistent gains (74\% vs 25\%) under full coupling---the method does not collapse.
    \item \textbf{Failure:} Pass/fail verification provides zero benefit; random remasking is destructive.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Verifier dependence.} The key requirement for effective repair is that the verifier produces nonzero local residuals on \emph{some} nonempty subset of corrupted positions. Our residual analysis shows that even 44\% precision suffices. This broadens the practical applicability beyond symbolic verifiers with perfect localization---noisy or partial verifiers are still useful.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our evaluation uses learned MLP denoisers that require task-specific training data. The denoiser-quality sweep shows that repair benefit collapses for denoisers below $\sim$30\% accuracy. Real-world deployment would require denoisers with adequate base accuracy. Additionally, our 10-step budget is generous for small domains; larger problems may require more iterations or hierarchical repair strategies.

\textbf{Relationship to prior work.} ReMDM~\cite{remdm2025} and RemeDi~\cite{remedi2025} use confidence-based remasking without external verifiers. Our pass/fail and random remask baselines show that non-localized signals provide zero benefit. Confidence-based remasking on coupled domains similarly fails (0\% in our experiments). The localized residual is the essential ingredient that distinguishes our approach.

% ==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We have shown that verifier-guided remasking with localized constraint residuals is a general and effective mechanism for inference-time repair in masked diffusion. The mechanism\'s benefit depends critically on the information content of the verifier signal: pass/fail verification provides no improvement, while localized residuals drive 45-point lifts. A coupling-density analysis across seven constraint-coupling levels shows that the method is robust to constraint interdependence and that even modest residual precision (44\%) suffices for meaningful repair.

Our results establish a scientific basis for verifier-guided remasking and open several directions: learned stopping policies for efficient repair, integration with approximate verifiers for real-world applications, and extension to domains like program repair where localizing errors is an open challenge.
